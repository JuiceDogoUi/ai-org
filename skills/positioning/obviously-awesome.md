# Obviously Awesome: 10-Step Positioning Process

## Prerequisites

- Assemble a cross-functional team (product, marketing, sales, customer success)
- Gather customer evidence: win/loss data, support tickets, testimonials, usage data
- Set aside assumptions about your current positioning

## Step 1: Understand Who Loves Your Product

Identify your best-fit customers — the ones who buy fast, renew, expand, and advocate. Look at usage data, NPS scores, expansion revenue, and unsolicited referrals. These customers reveal your true value, not the ones you wish you had.

## Step 2: Form a Positioning Team

Positioning is not a marketing exercise. It requires product, sales, and leadership. One person owns the process; the team owns the decision. Everyone who will execute on positioning needs to be in the room when it's defined.

## Step 3: Align on Vocabulary

Define what "positioning" means for your team. It is not messaging, taglines, or brand. It is the context that makes your value obvious to the right customers. Get agreement on this before proceeding.

## Step 4: List Competitive Alternatives

Ask: "What would our best customers use if we didn't exist?"

Include:
- **Direct competitors** — same category, same job
- **Indirect competitors** — different category, same job
- **Status quo** — spreadsheets, manual processes, doing nothing, hiring someone

Be honest. If your best customers would use Excel, list Excel.

## Step 5: Identify Unique Attributes

For each competitive alternative, list what you have that they do not. Be specific and provable:

- "Better UX" is not an attribute
- "Single-click deployment with zero CLI required" is an attribute
- "AI-powered" is not an attribute
- "Automated anomaly detection that reduces false positives by 80%" is an attribute

If you can't demonstrate it in a demo or prove it with data, it's not a unique attribute.

## Step 6: Map Attributes to Value

Each unique attribute enables a specific value for customers. Map them explicitly:

| Attribute | Value Enabled |
|-----------|--------------|
| Single-click deployment | Ship features in minutes, not hours |
| Built-in compliance engine | Pass audits without hiring consultants |
| Real-time collaboration | Eliminate version conflicts across teams |

Cluster related attribute-value pairs into **value themes**. Most products have 2-4 value themes.

## Step 7: Determine Target Customer Characteristics

Who cares most about the value themes from Step 6? Define characteristics, not demographics:

- What is their current situation?
- What makes them particularly receptive to your value?
- What do they have in common beyond industry and title?
- What makes them different from customers who don't care?

Good: "Teams that ship daily but lack dedicated DevOps"
Bad: "CTOs at mid-market SaaS companies"

## Step 8: Pick a Market Category

Choose the context that makes your value obvious. Three options:

- **Existing category** — compete head-on (requires clear differentiation)
- **Subcategory** — carve out a segment of an existing category (easier to own)
- **New category** — create a new frame (expensive, slow, high risk/reward)

**Category test**: Does a customer in this category immediately understand why your unique attributes matter? If you have to explain why your differentiators are relevant, you're in the wrong category.

## Step 9: Layer on Relevant Trends

Identify 1-2 trends that make your positioning more urgent and relevant NOW. Trends are tailwinds, not your positioning itself.

- "AI adoption in enterprises" is a trend
- "AI-powered analytics" is a category, not a trend
- "Remote-first teams" is a trend
- "Remote collaboration tool" is a category, not a trend

Trends answer: "Why now?" They make your positioning feel inevitable rather than optional.

## Step 10: Capture the Positioning

Document everything in the positioning canvas. Share across the organization. Positioning feeds into:

- **Messaging and copywriting** — landing pages, emails, ad copy
- **Sales narratives** — pitch decks, demo scripts, objection handling
- **Product roadmap** — prioritize what reinforces your unique value
- **Content strategy** — thought leadership that builds authority in your category
- **Hiring** — attract people who believe in your market category

## After Positioning

Positioning is the foundation, not the finish line. After completing the canvas:

1. **Test with sales** — does the positioning make conversations easier?
2. **Test with prospects** — does the category framing click immediately?
3. **Revisit quarterly** — has the competitive landscape shifted?
4. **Update when needed** — new capabilities, new competitors, or new market dynamics all trigger repositioning
